Structural transition

Legacy brands hold volume but all growth momentum sits in EV. The market is pivoting from ICE dominance to electrification driven by policy, not cost-optimization.

Infrastructure is the catalyst

Charging station density (importance 0.42) is the strongest predictor — far exceeding fuel price ratio. Range anxiety outweighs marginal savings from lower running costs.

Macro sensitivity

GDP growth and household debt (91.5% of GDP) carry high model coefficients — Thai vehicle demand is far more sensitive to economic health and credit conditions than operating costs.

Segment stickiness

The EV dummy variable showed the highest OLS significance — adoption is driven by segment preference (early adopters, subsidy recipients) not rational fuel-cost optimization.

Policy recommendations
  • 01
    Prioritize infrastructure over operating subsidies
    Government spending yields higher ROI if directed toward expanding the national charging grid — especially secondary cities — rather than subsidizing fuel or electricity costs. High impact
  • 02
    Green auto loans with subsidized interest rates
    Leasing flat rates and household debt are high-impact variables. Green Auto Loans with lower rates for EVs could mitigate debt headwinds and make EVs accessible to the middle class. Macro lever
  • 03
    Target subsidies at elastic brands — GWM and Toyota
    Demand-side subsidies (EV 3.5 package) are most effective on mass-market elastic brands. Luxury segments like Tesla show lower elasticity — subsidies there drive less total volume. Targeting
  • 04
    Standardize EV insurance frameworks
    EV premiums run 20–40% above ICE, acting as a hidden friction cost. A state-backed insurance pool or standardized battery repair costs could meaningfully lower total cost of ownership. TCO friction
